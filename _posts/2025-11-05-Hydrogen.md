# So, are we actually going to fly on Hydrogen?
I have just spent multiple days busting out an assignment on Hydrogen usage in commercial aircraft, so you are going to hear all about it.

The aviation industry is responsible for about 2.5% of global CO2 emissions as of 2023 (which is actually far lower than I expected but still not something to be prooud of). To keep the public from hating us and to actually meet targets like the Paris Agreement, everyone from Airbus to tiny startups are obsessed with hydrogen.

## The storage problem
Hydrogen is the guy who is great at a party but a nightmare to host. On one hand, it's gravimetric energy density is massive. On the other hand, its volumetric density is terrible. To get enough energy to actually move a plane, you have to turn it into a liquid at cryogenic temperatures.

This means we need massive, heavy, insulated tanks. These tanks take up a ton of space, ruin the aerodynamics, and kill the payload capacity. You could try other storage methods like metal hydrides, but those just make the plane even heavier, which isn't ideal when you're trying to not fall out of the sky.

## Burn baby burn
There are basically two ways to use hydrogen. The first is just modifying a regular gas turbine engine to burn hydrogen instead of kerosene. It’s faster to develop because we already know how engines work. It doesn't drop CO2, but it does still spit out nitrogen oxides (NOx) if the flame gets too hot. Also, unless we start building weirdly wide "blended wing" planes, these are only really good for short trips.

The second option is fuel cells. This is what Airbus has been focusing on since 2025. You react hydrogen with oxygen to make electricity, which then spins a propeller. It’s super quiet, very efficient at low power, and has zero emissions. The only byproduct is water. Fantastic, luckily there are no downsides right? The fuel cells, the batteries, and the cooling systems are incredibly heavy.

Pick your poison.

## Not even planes

Even if we build the perfect plane, we have to fuel it. Right now, most hydrogen is "grey," meaning it’s made from natural gas and still makes CO2. To be actually "green," we need to use renewable energy to electrocute the water. Currently, less than 0.1% of hydrogen is made this way because it’s expensive and hard to do at scale.

Plus, we can’t use old pipelines because they leak hydrogen. We need brand new, super-insulated refueling lines at airports and staff who are trained not to freeze their hands off or accidentally blow things up. Sounds like a civil engineers job to me.

## Verdict

Honestly, without some massive tech breakthroughs, a hydrogen-powered holiday to the other side of the world isn't happening tomorrow. But with the amount of money being thrown at it and the pressure to hit net zero by 2050, it’s not impossible. We just have a lot of research and development to do first.
